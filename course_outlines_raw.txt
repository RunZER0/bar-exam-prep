PART 1: COURSE DESCRIPTION

        This course seeks to acquaint the student with experiential learning of law from a skills-oriented perspective. The
        aim is to introduce students to development of legal skills and the professional approach essential to the practice
        of law and in particular Civil Litigation.                                  


        The course focuses on skill areas associated with Civil Litigation. A variety of teaching methods including formal,
        oral and written instruction, demonstrations, interactive group discussions, moots and other experiential learning
        exercises and assignments will be utilized to assist students develop efficiency in all skill areas. For this purpose,
        students will be expected to utilize their study group sessions, class presentations and moot courts extensively.

        The course advisors will endeavour to invite guest lecturers who are experienced in the practice of law to share
        their insights with the class on specific topics.                           

        It is emphasized that class attendance is of the essence and save for good cause, it is expected that every student
        will attend classes as scheduled on the timetable. Students are encouraged to consult as much as possible with
        the lecturers. They must also, of course, consult a wide variety of legal texts, articles, legislation and other relevant
        materials in the course of the academic year.

PART 2: PURPOSE OF THE SUBJECT

        This course equips the student with practical skills in resolution of civil disputes.

PART 3: EXPECTED LEARNING OUTCOMES

By the end of this subject, the learner should be able to:
        i) Perform effective client conferencing and taking of instructions         
        ii) Apply the knowledge garnered to advise clients on appropriate strategies to resolve civil disputes,
        iii) Demonstrate a proper knowledge of the law, court rules and practice that apply in civil litigation,

        iv) Represent clients in dispute resolution processes in a just, efficient, proportionate and cost-effective manner,
        v) Apply professional ethics and standards in civil litigation.             

PART 4: TEACHING AND INSTRUCTIONAL OBJECTIVES
The teaching and instructional objectives of the course are to:


        i) to ensure the students’ understanding of the practical application of the Civil Procedure Act (cap 21) and the
          Civil Procedure Rules, 2010 made thereunder, the Court of Appeal Rules, 2022, the Supreme Court Rules,
          2012, and related statutes                                                
        ii) to ensure that a student has a firm grasp of the multifaceted Rules of Civil Procedure, 2010, and other Rules

          of Court and how they apply in Kenyan courts;                             
        iii) instill in the student a firm understanding and the practical application of the Civil Procedure Act and the
          Civil Procedure Rules, 2010 and other Court Rules and relevant statutes, and illustrative case-law to a given
          set of facts;                                                             
        iv) to ensure that the student is equipped with the necessary interpretational and practical application skills for

          an effective civil litigation advocate,                                   
        v) to provide clinical training, including simulations and moot courts      
        vi) to emphasize relevant constitutional provisions, and the prescribed Overriding Objective, in civil justice
          administration in Kenya.

INTRODUCTION
            Criminal litigation deals with procedural aspects of the application of Criminal Law. The basic Act of

            Parliament that sets out provisions to be followed in criminal litigation is the Criminal Procedure Code, Chapter
            75 of the Laws of Kenya.                                                

            All students are encouraged to read the requirements of the course outline and beyond its confines and also be
            conversant with the Constitution, basic principles of Criminal Liability, contents of the Penal Code (Chapter 63

            of the Laws of Kenya) and the Evidence Act (Chapter 80 Laws of Kenya). Course content will integrate and
            emphasise the new constitutional order promulgated on 27 August 2010. In the meantime relevant legislation will

            continue forming part of reference with necessary modifications in keeping with the new constitutional order. Instructions
            will be dynamic in keeping with developments in the law                 


PURPOSE OF THE SUBJECT
             The Purpose of the subject is to equip learners with knowledge and skills in the criminal process
             and procedure.

EXPECTED LEARNING OUTCOMES
             By the end of the subject, the learner should be able to:-             

                i. Draft documents relevant in criminal litigation.                 
               ii. Identify various jurisdictions in criminal litigation;           
               iii. Apply substantive and procedural law relating to criminal trials;
               iv. Demonstrate case management skills in criminal litigation.

2. COURSE METHOD
         Instructions will be conveyed in two methods. The Socratic method of teaching will be used to introduce
         concepts in each topic covered. Thereafter the experiential method of learning (learning by doing) will be
         utilised to build on the skill set necessary for effective legal writing and drafting.

         Exercises will be given to the class after every lecture, which must be discussed and finalised at firm
         meetings or such other time as the firms may create for that purpose. All firms must be ready to present
         their work for discussion the following week.                              
         Any member of the firm may be picked on by the instructor to make the presentation on behalf of the
         group. This means that attendance at preparatory meetings is compulsory. In order to give the student as

         much writing practice as possible, significant archival research will be required. Researching the applicable
         law is essential in any legal writing and drafting work.                   

3. CLASS ATTENDANCE

         Attendance and participation of a student at lectures and firm meetings is required. Participation consists
of more than mere attendance. Actual participation occurs when you:

           •  Show you have prepared by reading and thinking about assigned material.
           •  Show you understand or are trying to understand the course concepts and principles.
           •  Ask questions and build on other students’ contributions.             
           •  Exhibit willingness to experiment with new skills.                    

         The student is particularly encouraged to participate as fully as possible in class and during firm meetings.

         Ask questions where any issue is not clear. Do not let any opportunity to ask a question or contribute to a
         discussion pass by. This way the student will not only understand concepts but also build upon oral
         communication (advocacy) skills.

         Statement of Course Philosophy, Goals, Delivery and Assessment             

         Introduction                                                               
         As with other professions, the legal profession is governed by standards of professional practice and ethical conduct.

         For example, the Advocates Act, Chapter 16 regulates the practice of law and punishes professional misconduct as
         a disciplinary offence. These standards protect clients while upholding the profession's esteem and preserving
         judicial authority.                                                        

         This course, therefore, covers the rules of professional conduct for the legal profession in Kenya. The overall aim is
         to introduce students to a high standard for conduct that preserves the rule of law, public confidence, and the dignity

         of the legal profession. We will ground our study in the Constitution, the Advocates Act and Practice Rules, the
         Law Society of Kenya’s codes, and leading case law, while always connecting doctrine to the everyday ethical
         decisions advocates must make.                                             

         Students will explore the overriding values and principles that define an advocate’s professional life: integrity,

         independence, confidentiality, loyalty, candour to the court, and responsible public engagement. Students will then
         consider the Standard of Professional Practice and Ethical Conduct (“SOPPEC”) for advocates in Kenya alongside
         other ethical guidelines given by the Law Society of Kenya.                
                                            1                                       






         Emphasis will be placed on how these standards protect clients, ensure the fair administration of justice, and sustain
         the profession’s image and social license. We will examine tensions—such as client privilege versus the public

         interest—and the practical rules that resolve them.                        

         You will be expected to internalize the rules and, thereafter, to act with courage when ethics demand it, to decline
         or withdraw from matters where conflicts or impropriety arise, and to prioritize the public good when the law and
         conscience require. Continuous professional development, anti-money laundering, respectful courtroom decorum

         in virtual court, and the ethical use of modern tools, including social media and artificial intelligence, will be
         explored.                                                                  

         Through case studies, drafting exercises, and reflective assessments, the course will equip you to identify ethical
         dilemmas, apply the governing rules, justify professional decisions, and propose reforms when practice falls short.
         By the end of the course, you will be able to articulate the ethical obligations of an advocate in Kenya and model

         those obligations in your practice.                                        

         Purpose of the Subject                                                     

         To introduce students to the rules of professional and ethical standards in Kenya.

Expected Learning Outcomes:

By the end of the subject, the student should:


            i.   Identify the rules, laws, regulations and codes of Professional Ethics and Practice governing the
                 conduct of legal professionals in Kenya.                           
            ii.  Apply the rules of Professional Ethics to practical scenarios.     
            iii. Explain their chosen course of action in any given ethical dilemma using relevant principles.
            iv.  Draft selected documents on topical issues of Professional Ethics

         Some of the material will be presented using PowerPoint presentation slides. For effective discussion, case law and
         relevant readings (statutes, journal articles, etc.) will be covered under the course instructor's guidance. As such,
         prior reading is recommended to prepare for a class. For some topics, guest lecturers will be brought to teach

         alongside the course instructor. All students will be required to actively participate in group discussions and class
         presentations.

         Regular and keen class attendance is important for understanding, following, and completing the course

A. INTRODUCTION
          This Course is aimed at furnishing students with the necessary tools to enable them establish and / or
          manage a law practice. Students are expected to keep in touch with what is happening in legal practice
          both locally and internationally. They need to appreciate the effect of Constitutional and other legal
          developments in Kenya; coupled with the effect of globalization and its impact on legal practice and
          management of legal resources. The market is fairly competitive but we expect our students to have a
          competitive edge in this ever-changing environment. The needs and expectations of the client, in today’s
          society, are more challenging than ever due to advancements in ICT and unprecedented increase in legal
          awareness. Upon completion of this course, students are expected to seamlessly fit in legal practice and
          confidently serve their clients who have diverse needs that require understanding of modern
          management practice.

PURPOSE OF THE UNIT
          To equip the learner with practical legal skills in commercial transactions

EXPECTED LEARNING OUTCOMES

By the end of the unit the learner should be able to:
            1. Explain and apply the procedures required in processing commercial transaction
               documents                                                            
            2. Draft different types of commercial agreements                       
            3. Apply the relevant law in solving disputes in commercial transactions


          Learning and Teaching Methods                                             
          i. Lectures                                                               
          ii. Moot courts                                                           

          iii. Role plays                                                           
          iv. Use of Audio visual aids                                              
          v. Guest lectures

               Learning Objectives

TOPIC 2. MERGERS, ACQUISITIONS AND TAKE-OVERS
               Learning Objectives                                                  
By the end of the topic the learner should be able to:

                 a) Assess and apply the due diligence process in M&A transactions. 
                 b) Understand the negotiation and documentation involved in M&A deals.

               Required Statutes and Reference Materials

TOPIC 3. INSOLVENCY PROCEDURES

               Learning Objectives                                                  
By the end of the topic the learner should be able to:
                 a) Understand the legal principles and procedures governing insolvency and bankruptcy.
                 b) Identify the legal framework governing insolvency in Kenya      
                 c) Analyze the rights and obligations of creditors and debtors in insolvency proceedings
                 d) Evaluate the role of insolvency practitioners and the court in managing insolvency cases.
                 e) Identify the forms required for insolvency petitions            

               Required Statutes and Reference Materials                            
                 a) The Insolvency Act 2015                                         
                 b) The Banking Act CAP 488 and the various amendments              
                 c) The Insurance Act                                               

                 d) Kenya Deposit Insurance act 2012                                
                 e) Central Bank Act                                                
                 f) https://brs.go.ke/wp-content/uploads/2023/07/Debtors-Bankruptcy-Proceedings.pdf

TOPIC 5. COMMERCIAL AGREEMENTS
               Learning Objectives                                                  
By the end of the topic the learner should be able to:
                 a) Understand the key elements and legal principles of commercial contracts.
                 b) Analyze the negotiation, drafting, and enforcement of commercial agreements.
                 c) Evaluate the remedies available for breach of commercial contracts.


TOPIC 6. MOVABLE PROPERTY SECURITY RIGHTS
               Learning Objectives                                                  
By the end of the topic the learner should be able to:
                 a) Understand the legal framework governing security interests in movable property.
                 b) Analyze the creation, perfection, and enforcement of security rights.
                 c) Evaluate the rights and obligations of secured parties and debtors.

                 c) Evaluate the role of tax authorities and the court in resolving tax disputes.
